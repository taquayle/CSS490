Integrating Simple Broad Phase Collision

 

Due time: Please refer to our course web-site

 

Objective

In this programming assignment we will experience integrating a functional physics framework into our own game engine via integrating the broad phase collision detection and the drawing of physics bounds.

 

 

Assignment Specification:

Here is an example of the results from running this assignment:

Your scene will include a hero and five enemies.

The hero: The hero defines a RigidRectangle shape for future participation with our Physics component. The RigidRectangle is bounded by a circle for broad phase collision detection. Here are the details:

         Drawing the RigidRectangle and the circle bound:

o    Each edge of the RigidRectangle must be drawn with a unique color

o    The face normal associated with an edge must also be drawn in the same color, and located at one of the vertices of the edge

o    The bounding circle for the RigidRectangle must be drawn in white color

o    Renderable size: 9x12

o    RigidRectangle size: 9x12

         User Control:

o    WASD will move the hero accordingly

o    ZX: rotates the hero counterclockwise/clockwise with respect to the center of the hero object.

 

The enemies: The enemies define a RigidCircle shape for future participation with our Physics component. The RigidCircle is bounded by a circle for broad phase collision detection. Here are the details:

         Drawing the RigidCircle and the circle bound:

o    The RigidCircle should be drawn in black

o    The bounding circle for the RigidCircle must be drawn in white color

o    Note: by default the RigidCirlce and the bounding circle size are the same and thus you will only see one of the circles. This default can be adjusted by the arrow keys as detailed below.

o    Renderable size: 12x9.6

o    RigidCircle radius size: 4

         Behavior:

o    Moves with a speed of around 20 units per second in random direction.

  When an enemys transform bound touches the WC bound, it reflects.

o    Rotates with respect to its center at about 0.6 rad per second

  An enemy will not rotate more than 1 rad from its original position

o    Enemies are in perpetual motion/rotation

 

The world: My world is defined in a 800x600 viewport, with world coordinate width of 100. Here are the details of the world:

         Collision: All RigidShape in the world must participate in continuous broad phase collision

o    When a collision is detected, all colliding shapes will result in flipping their velocities.

o    Note:

  The Hero has no speed, thus, flipping its velocity does not result in any visible behavior changes

  When there is a significant interpenetration between traveling enemies, the colliding Enemies may resulting in continuous flipping of their velocities over subsequent frames. As a result, it will appear as though the overlapping enemies are not moving.

         User control:

o    T Key: toggles the displaying of the texture

o    Left/Right arrow keys: selects the current object (cycles form 0: the hero, through all the enemies and back to 0 again)

o    Up/Down arrow keys: increase/decrease the bounding circles radius

  For the enemies, this is why you will observe the difference between the RigidCircle and bounding circle. Note: all collisions are detected by the broad phase method, and thus at the bounds of the bounding circle (the white circles)

         Echo status:

o    Echo the current selected object: as a number

o    Echo the size of the bounding circle for the selected object

 

Hint:

         This assignment is not difficult, but requires you to understand both of the system: our game engine and the physics engine.

o    Make sure to read and understand Example 3.1 (Broad Phase Method) from the Physics Book.

o    My implementation is based on Example 7.6 (Line Support) from our Engine Book.

         Strategy: we need to remember physics component in our Game Engine context, is simply another component to the GameObject (just like Renderable is a component of the GameObject). As such, in my implementation:

o    GameObject defines a mPhysicsComponent: by default it is null, you can either assign a RigidCircle, or RigidRectangle to this variable.

o    I define a new gEngine.Physics engine component to house the physics computation (to house the code in EngineCore/Physics.js of the Engine book).

o    You must differentiate between RigidShape collision and GameObject.mXform.Bound interaction with Camera.WC.Bound.

         When integrating my RigidShape:

o    mCenter is the XForm.position of the GameObject

o    mAngle is the XForm.rotation of the GameObject

         Note the math library difference (assuming A, B, and C: vectors, and t is a scalar):

o    In the Physics book:

  A = vec2.add(B,C),

  A = vec2.add(B, C * t)

o    In the Engine book:

  vec2.add(A, B, C);

  Vec2.scaleAndAdd(A, B, C, t)

         I have added this function to lib/gl-matrix.js: to rotate a point (aPoint) with respect to a reference position (refPt):

 

vec2.rotateWRT(out, aPoint, angleInRadian, refPt)
// rotate aPoint by angleInRadian, WRT refPt

Here is the implementation

vec2.rotateWRT = function(out, a, c, pt){

var r=[];

vec2.subtract(r, a, pt);

vec2.rotate(r, r, c);

vec2.add(r, r, pt);

out[0] = r[0];

out[1] = r[1];

 

return r;

};
